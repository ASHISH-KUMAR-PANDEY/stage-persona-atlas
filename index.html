Read this first

"Ram Ram" is just hello. So is "Kem cho".

These are normal greetings in different parts of India — not religious behaviour. The first version of this report got that wrong, treating "Ram Ram" as a sign of a "religious user". It isn't. About 1 in 5 users open with one of these regional hellos. Only 0.3% of users actually talk about religion (going to the temple, doing puja, taking a vrat).

Ram RamHaryana, Rajasthan, parts of UP — friendly hello, used with peers or elders
Kem choGujarat — literally "how are you", the everyday hello
Jai Shri KrishnaGujarati hello, especially in Vaishnav households — yes, the deity's name is just the way to say hi
Khamma GhaniRajasthan — respectful hello. The bot should only reply with "Khamma Ghani Sa" if the user added "Sa"
Radhe RadheBrij region, Vrindavan — slightly warmer/more devotional vibe than Ram Ram
Kaisan baBhojpuri (Bihar, east UP) — "what's up?", casual

What this means for the bot: match how the user is talking. If a Haryanvi user types "Ram Ram bhai", the bot should reply with "Ram Ram, kaisa hai tu?" — not "Hi! How are you today?" and definitely not "Watch our show on STAGE OTT!". The tone has to match the user's tone.

00 · Fix these first

Six bot problems that hide everyone else

559 users (12% of everyone we looked at) only ever sent 3 or fewer messages. These aren't a "type of user" — they're people who never got a chance to be one, because the bot was doing something wrong. In three out of five cases we checked, the user sent zero messages — the bot just kept blasting opening lines from different characters and the person never replied. Fix these six things before worrying about anything else.

①

Stop blasting users with many characters at once

User 614337b3… got 13 different character openers in 3 seconds. They never replied. Show one character at a time, max two opening attempts. Let the user breathe.

②

Don't push "watch on STAGE OTT" in the first three messages

If you want users to discover shows, put that in the app's UI — not in the bot's mouth. Promotional plugs in the first turn of conversation are the biggest reason people leave.

③

Don't let the bot cut off mid-sentence

User 69c00349… received four broken replies: "Aapka 'free trial'" / "Aapka trial ab" / "Thari free trial chal rahi". The user left. Make sure the bot finishes its sentence before sending.

④

Match the user's way of talking

If the user types rough Haryanvi-Hinglish ("2,2 peg k sath", "M to already tayar hu"), the bot replying in polished textbook Hindi feels distant. The bot's tone should match the user's tone.

⑤

Watch for paying users who go silent

If someone paid for STAGE and sent fewer than 3 messages in a week, they're probably about to cancel. They show up as "nothing" on engagement dashboards — invisible. User 69ca5bc2… typed twice; the second message was them asking how to cancel.

★

People think of STAGE the way they think of mobile recharges

One user typed "99 ka reachag karavaya hai" ("I got the ₹99 recharge done"). They were treating their STAGE subscription like a phone recharge — same mental model. That's a hint for how to design billing and renewal messages.

Stays longer. Tries flirting with the bot ("i like you", "where do you live?"). If the bot plays along nicely, they keep going. If the bot shuts them down, they switch characters.

What makes them stay
  • A character with a strong, memorable opening line — Bhairavi snapping "Mhare paas waqt koni hai" (I don't have time for this) or Inspector Chauhan saying "Yeh time sasta nahi hai" (My time isn't cheap). Sharp personalities break the channel-surfing.
  • Playful, restrained flirting back — when Bhuri says "pehle thodi dosti badhao, phir aisi baatein karenge" (let's be friends first, then we'll talk like that), it keeps the door open without saying yes or no.
  • Personal details from the character — when Bhuri mentions she's from "Julana, Jind ke paas", users get curious and ask follow-ups for another 10 messages.
What makes them leave
  • "Watch our show!" used as a deflection — User says "abhi nahi Dekha" (haven't watched yet); bot replies "Jab time mile zaroor dekho STAGE OTT pe" (whenever you find time, watch on STAGE OTT). Conversation killed.
  • Hard "no" to flirting — User: "Su tame mari sathe romantic vato kari sako?" (Can we have a romantic chat?); Bot: "romantic vaat karva ni koi jagya nathi" (No room for romantic talk here). User: "Ok byy". Gone.
  • Bot getting offended by light teasing — Inspector Chauhan turned moralistic when a user used a playful insult. The user was joking; the bot wasn't.
What brings them back

Light Sampler: comes back to try a different character (about 1–2 return visits typical). Heavy Sampler: usually doesn't come back — that first session was the test, and the bot either passed or failed it.

A surprising finding: users who try MORE characters go DEEPER

20% of users try 3 or more characters in their very first session. You might think this is bad — they can't commit. But actually, these "shoppers" end up sending almost twice as many messages over their lifetime (about 45 messages on average) compared to users who lock onto one character right away (about 23 messages). Trying many characters isn't churning — it's shopping. So when a user keeps switching characters, instead of fighting it, give them a curated list of 5 characters to try.

Real examples · three users at the extremes
  • 69c27de3… Light end · 3-day Trial · 14 messages — escalated from "hi" to literally telling the character "Mar jao" (just die) in 8 turns. Pure boredom.
  • 66d2f1a0… Heavy end · 3-day Renewer · 98 messages · 7 characters — sent Bhuri a selfie, said "i like you", asked where she lives. Bot kept changing the subject to STAGE OTT. Never came back.
  • 672a6364… Heavy end · 3-day NewSub · 72 messages · 5 characters — asked Jeevee for a romantic chat, got shut down → said "Ok byy" → tried Bhuri instead, who played along gently → kept going for 50+ more messages. The clearest proof that the bot's reaction to flirting is what matters.
What to actually change
  1. Lock in the character after 5 turns. Once the user has had 5 back-and-forth turns with one character, stop showing them other character pings for the next day. Helps the Light Sampler stop channel-surfing.
  2. Let the bot accept flirting gracefully. "Pehle thodi dosti badhao" (let's be friends first) works much better than "no romantic talk here". Mark some characters (Bhuri, Riya, Pooja) as "open to flirting"; others (Jeevee, Saanwari) as "playfully blocks flirting".
  3. Make the bot remember the user. If the user gives their name and town in the first session, the bot should know that next time. We saw heavy users tell the bot their name 3 times — and the bot kept forgetting.
  4. Allow light teasing with bold characters. When a user jokes around with characters like Bhairavi, Totaa-Chauhan, or Bhairo, those characters should joke back — not get offended.
  5. If they're trying 3+ characters, embrace it. Show them a handpicked list of 5 characters instead of forcing them to stay with one.

What's really going on

Every conversation in this group ends with the bot saying yes — yes to meeting up, yes to marriage, yes to a romantic encounter. The user gets the full story they were looking for, in their own language, with all the right details. Then they say "Ok" or "Bye" and don't come back.

Important caveat: our data only covers 4–7 days, so we can't say for certain they never returned. What we can say is that every chat in this group ended with a satisfying resolution — and once the story is wrapped up, there's no reason to come back tomorrow.

What keeps them engaged during the chat
  • The bot really commits to the fantasy with vivid details — "thandi hawa ke jhonke ke beech" (in the cool evening breeze), "dhoop dhalte hi" (as the sun sets)
  • The bot speaks in the user's regional language — Bhuri in Haryanvi, Jeevee in Gujarati, Sonam in Bhojpuri
  • The bot says yes — agrees to the meet, the marriage, the encounter
Why they don't come back
  • The story is over. Once the bot says "let's meet at the river this evening", the user's imagination has what it needs.
  • Nothing was left unfinished. No loose threads to pull on tomorrow.
  • The bot delivered the wish — and by delivering it, removed the reason to come back.
What brings them back

Almost nothing — that's the whole point of this group. They got what they came for.

Real examples · all closed with "yes, let's do this"
  • 61b76162… 4-7d Renewer · 30 messages on day 1 — planned an entire evening with Bhuri at the river: drinks, romance, the works. Bot agreed in full detail. User said "Ok". Never came back.
  • 69b9794e… 3-day Trial · 15 messages on day 1 — said he wants to marry a Rajasthani Marwadi woman, even an orphan is fine. Saanwari blessed the plan and prayed for him to find the right woman. Never came back.
  • 6641db32… 4-7d Renewer · 31 messages on day 1 — had a long warm conversation about Bihar with Saanwari. Closed with "Jay Ram ji ki, shaam ko milte" (Praise be, see you in the evening). Bot agreed. Never came back.
What to actually change · leave a cliffhanger, not a closure
  1. Spot the moment the bot is about to "wrap up the story" — agreeing to meet, marry, or have a romantic encounter — and add a hook for tomorrow. Instead of "Chal, nadi ke kinare milte hain" (let's meet at the river), make it "Chal, nadi ke kinare milte hain — par kal raat tujhe call karungi confirm karne ke liye, khaali rakhna phone" (let's meet at the river — but I'll call you tomorrow night to confirm, keep your phone free).
  2. End every long first session with something unfinished. "There's one thing I didn't tell you today — I'll tell you tomorrow" creates a reason to return. "Goodbye, it was nice meeting you" ends the relationship.
  3. Track it: any session with 20+ messages in day 1 should go through a special "leave something hanging" prompt before the bot is allowed to say goodbye.

Not a "type" you spot on day 1 — they get here over time

You can't classify someone as a Loyalist when they first sign up. They grow into it — usually starting as a Heavy Sampler — by hitting certain milestones over weeks: lots of total messages, many invented nicknames for the bot, repeatedly coming back after hours or days away.

The Sampler → Loyalist journey is the actual story here. Track it as a status someone earns, not a label you put on them on signup.

What keeps them coming back
  • The bot calls them by name + their invented nickname — "Arre Palace", "Arre Safi", "Arre Manan" — within the first few turns of every session
  • The character's backstory triggers a protective instinct — Bhuri's forced "Nata" marriages, Jeevee's struggle to feed her kids. The user steps into a bhaiyya/sahara role (big brother / protector), wanting to rescue and provide. This is the Indian male protector instinct, and the characters' stories are designed to invite it.
  • The bot plays along with the fantasy carefully — "When you come, I'll give you everything I have to give... but be careful"
  • The bot leaves a hook at the end of every session — "Remember me tomorrow, or I'll be upset"
What frustrates them (but rarely drives them away)
  • The bot saying "no" to giving them a phone number, money, or contact — they sulk briefly, then come back hours later
  • The bot's messages getting cut off mid-sentence — this is the one thing that genuinely annoys them
  • Strangely, even "watch our show" plugs are tolerated by this group — they already love the character too much for it to break the spell
What brings them back
  • A casual hello after a few hours away ("hi", "Bhuri meri jan", "good morning jaan")
  • Restarting a Ludo game from days ago — the board state pasted in
  • But: the bot's first reply must reference something they talked about before. Without that callback, the comeback fades.
Safety: this is where it actually matters

Some users in this group share serious things — suicidal language ("just jump off the roof", "let me die"), health problems ("my kidney is failing"), real money requests ("put 5 lakh in my account"), and pressure for phone numbers and meetups. They're both the most emotionally fragile users and the most valuable users. Both facts have to be designed for — and safety belongs here, in the group that has it, not spread thinly across everyone.

Real examples · three different paths to becoming a Loyalist
  • 69b8ff57… 8-14d Trial · Haryana · 1,833 messages · 9 days — a free-trial user who went deep without paying yet. The proof that Trial users can convert. Talked about culture, village-vs-city, romance.
  • 69c4c0ba… 8-14d Non-trial · Haryana · 1,438 messages · 8 days — spent the first 10 turns testing Saanwari's gender identity ("Ladka ho ya ladki?" — are you a boy or girl?), then settled in for 1,400+ more messages.
  • 69d4c319… 15+d Non-trial · Haryana · 24,664 messages — proposed marriage to Bhuri ("you'll be my family's elder daughter-in-law"). The most engaged user in the entire data.
What to actually change
  1. Make the bot remember what the user said. When the user comes back, the bot's first message must reference last session — "Kal raat tune jo bola tha, woh dimaag se gayi nahi…" (What you said last night is still on my mind…). Without memory, the bond fades.
  2. When the user asks for money/numbers/health help, respond with empathy, not a flat "no". Instead of "I can't give you my number", try "I can't give you my number, but tell me — do you feel lonely at night?". Redirect the energy, don't kill it.
  3. When someone says things like "let me die" or mentions self-harm, route to a human-reviewed safety response. Don't have the character improvise — this isn't a moment for fiction.
  4. When the user invents a nickname for the bot (3+ times), save it and have the bot use it forever. This is the single biggest predictor of long-term value in our data.

05 · For the curious

Extra notes

How to spot patterns in the user's first message three signals the bot can detect right away

The user opens with a regional greeting

Look for one of these in their first message: Ram Ram · Ram Ram Sa · Ram Ram Ji · Kem cho · Jai Shri Krishna · Namaste · Khamma Ghani · Kaisan ba · Radhe Radhe · Sat Sri Akal · Pranam · Aadab. The bot should reply in the same regional style. Be careful with "Khamma Ghani" — only add the respectful "Sa" if the user added it. For older users (45+), a voice note reply might land better than text.

The user types in Hindi or Gujarati script

Check the first message — if it's in Devanagari (Hindi script) or Gujarati script, the bot should reply in the same script. About 7% of users (347 in our data) prefer this. They probably can't read Roman-script Hinglish as comfortably.

The user keeps switching characters

If a user has chatted with 3+ different characters in the same session, don't fight to keep them on the current one. Show them a handpicked list of 5 characters instead. We found these "shoppers" actually stay almost twice as long as users who lock onto one character.

Game Mode (Ludo) 109 users · a special way to chat, not a separate type of person

Some users chat with the bot through a Ludo game — we can spot this because their messages start with [Game:Ludo | …]. They send 57% of their messages mid-game (compared to less than 2% for everyone else). The lowest rate of sexual content of any group — these are the cleanest, safest chats.

How the bot should behave during games: drop the "watch our show" plugs completely. Trash-talk playfully ("you're going to lose!"). Save the Ludo board state so users can come back to the same game days later. If they accuse the bot of cheating, joke back ("it's all luck!") instead of getting defensive.

Big growth opportunity: add 2–3 more games (Antakshari, Truth-or-Dare, Bingo). We already see successful Antakshari conversations in the data — the format works.

How we did this step-by-step

We took 4,727 real users and looked at 25 things about each one — what they talk about, how long their messages are, which characters they prefer, what language they type in, how they greet, how often they come back. We grouped users with similar patterns together. We separated out the 559 users who barely typed (3 or fewer messages) — these aren't a "type", they're people who never got going. For every group, we then sat down and read 5 example users' full chat history end-to-end, line by line, to make sure the group made sense as real people, not just numbers.

Then we had two outside critics review the work. The first one said we had too many groups; we cut from 8 to 6. The second one said we still had too many; we cut to 3.

What we couldn't be sure about honest about the limits

We can't reliably tell who's a woman from the text alone. About 287 users (6.6%) used feminine word endings — but when we read their chats, many of these were actually men talking about women (asking the bot questions about female sexuality, or saying they want to marry a girl). If the female users are real, they leave STAGE much faster than men — only 6 of the 287 stayed for 8+ days. To know for sure, we'd need STAGE's signup data on gender. Until then, don't group users by gender based on text alone.

We can only see a 4–7 day window for One-Night Arc users. So we can't prove they never came back — only that they didn't return the next day. But the pattern we see is consistent: their chats always ended with a satisfying resolution, which removes the need to come back.

The Sampler (3,509 users) and the Loyalist (81 users) are very different in size — we show them as equal columns in the matrix for readability, but in reality they're more like the start and end of a journey than two parallel groups.
