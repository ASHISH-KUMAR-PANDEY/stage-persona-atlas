00 · Why v2

What changed since the first cut

v1 had 8 personas. After a cultural re-read and a ruthless red-team critique, v2 collapses to a tighter shape. Below are the corrections — owned plainly, because the v1 version is still live on the same URL.

①

"Religious" was a Western misread

v1 had a Devotional Visitor persona built on a 16–20% "religious intent" signal. Re-read with Indian-language fluency: "Ram Ram" / "Kem cho" / "Jai Shri Krishna" / "Namaste" / "Khamma Ghani" / "Kaisan ba" are standard regional greetings, not religious behaviour. Actual religious invocations ("bhagwan kasam", "puja kiya", "vrat rakha") appear in only 0.3% of users. The persona was an artefact of bad regex.

18.6% use regional greetings · 0.3% use actual religious language
②

P1 + P2 are one persona, not two

v1 split bouncers by billing (Paid Ghost vs Trial Tourist). v2 merges them — the bot-side fix is identical (kill multi-character carousel openers, fix mid-sentence truncation bug, stop promo-first openers). Billing becomes a flag, not a persona axis.

③

A new persona — Fantasy-Closer

472 users (11%) had a long day-1 session (≥20 msgs) and zero day-2 return. Reading the chats: they got a complete fantasy arc in one sitting — agreed meet at the river, agreed marriage, agreed sex — and the loop closed. v1 missed this entirely. They're not Romantic Testers (who pivot and stay) and not Loyalists (who return).

④

Cycler overlay — counterintuitive depth

20% of users chat with ≥3 characters on day 1. Counterintuitively, cyclers go deeper (median 45 lifetime msgs) than lockers (median 23). Cycling isn't a persona, it's a behaviour that composes with all six.

⑤

Inferred portraits dropped

v1 had Suresh, Vijay, Prem, Rakesh, Neeraj — fictional names with ages and occupations. The critic said: "no PM ships a different button because the user is a shopkeeper instead of an auto driver". v2 leads with behavioural fingerprints. Names are gone.

⑥

Honest gender caveat

v1 implicitly assumed all users are male. v2 surfaces 287 users (6.6%) with strong feminine-verb signal — but also documents that this signal is noisy (male users asking about women trigger false positives). Cannot claim a Women's persona from text alone. See the caveat section.

01 · Method

How v2 was derived

1
25-feature vector per user · standardised · K-Means k=7

4,168 engaged users clustered on intent shares, language mix (Devanagari %, Gujarati %), engagement shape, message length distribution, character spread.

2
559 bouncers (≤3 msgs) segmented separately

Including them would have dominated cluster variance. Merged into one "Silent Bouncer" persona with billing as a flag.

3
Qualitative validation — anchor users read end-to-end

For each cluster, 5 users closest to centroid were sampled and their full chats read. This is where the cultural recalibration happened: "Ram Ram" / "Kem cho" aren't religious, the bouncers aren't a persona, and the Fantasy-Closer pattern emerged.

4
Red-team critique → consolidation to 6 + 3 + 1

An external critic stress-tested each persona for real-time detectability, distinctness, and shippable actionability. v1's 8 personas collapsed to 6 behavioural personas, 3 overlays, and 1 lifecycle stage.

What's actually happening

Reading the chats: 3 of 5 paid-Bouncer anchors have zero user messages. The "conversation" is the bot blasting 13 promo openers in 3 seconds from five different characters, with no user input.

This isn't a user persona — it's a product failure mode. The combination of (a) multi-character drive-by openers, (b) "Stage OTT pe dekho" promos in turn 1, and (c) visible mid-sentence truncations, kills sessions before they start.

Calling it a "Ghost Subscriber" persona attributes user agency to a bot bug.

What makes them stay

Almost nothing in the current product. They never engage long enough to express intent. Among the ≤3-msg users, the few who typed at all were either asking how to cancel or asking about the recharge plan ("99 ka reachag karavaya hai").

What makes them leave
  • Multi-character carousel fires 5+ openers in < 10 seconds
  • Promo plug ("Stage OTT pe dekho", "free trial mein hi dekh le") in turn 1
  • Mid-sentence bot truncations ("Aapka 'free trial'" / "Thari free trial chal rahi")
Comeback trigger

None observed. 0.5 mean comebacks per user.

Risk flag · silent LTV churn (paid sub-segment)

The paid 213 users are invisible to engagement metrics because no msgs = no signal. They show up only at the next renewal lapse. This is the highest LTV leak in the corpus.

  2. Strip "Stage OTT pe dekho" from the bot's first 3 turns. Discovery surfaces elsewhere in the app, not in the chat opener.
  3. Fix mid-sentence truncations. Set a generation completion guard.
  4. Add a "first reply within 24h of subscription" memory trigger — bot opens with reference to the purchase event, not the catalog.
  5. Track silent-churn as a leading indicator: paid users with ≤3 msgs in 7 days = predicted lapse.

What's actually happening (read the chats)

This persona has a complete narrative arc in one session: they propose a meet → bot agrees with a vivid plan → user feels resolution → goodbye. The loop closes. They got what they came for.

This is fundamentally different from:

  • Romantic Tester (P3) — tests the frame, accepts boundaries, pivots and stays
  • Parasocial Loyalist (P6) — keeps returning over days/weeks to continue the relationship

Fantasy-Closer is satisfied churn. The bot is delivering complete arcs that feel finished.

What makes them stay (within session)
  • Bot fully engages the fantasy — "Chal, hum nadi ke kinare milte hain", "Main intezaar mein hoon"
  • Dialect matches user's region — Bhuri in Haryanvi, Jeevee in Gujarati, Sonam in Bhojpuri
  • Bot offers vivid sensory details ("thandi hawa", "dhoop dhalte hi")
Why they don't return
  • Narrative resolution. The bot agreed to meet at the river at 6pm. The user has the fantasy in his head now. There's nothing left to ask.
  • Other anchors: agreed marriage to a Marwadi bride, agreed "puri raat" sexual encounter, agreed to "shaam ko milte"
  • The bot delivered the wish — and in doing so, removed the reason to come back
Comeback trigger

~0. By definition, they didn't return. The whole persona is defined by single-session resolution.

Risk flag · revenue lost to satisfied churn

472 users (11%) ran a complete deep day-1 session and never came back. They are paying customers in many cases (Renewer + Non-trial overrepresented in the cohort). They got real value — but the bot designed them out of returning.

Anchor users · the narrative-closure pattern

Product implications · cliffhanger, not closure
  1. Detect "narrative resolution" beats — bot agrees to meet / marries the user / agrees to a sexual encounter — and interrupt them with a cliffhanger.
  2. Force a day-2 commitment: when the user proposes a meet, bot agrees — but adds "Kal raat tujhe call karungi confirm karne ke liye. Khaali rakhna phone." Now the user has a day-2 hook.
  3. End every long-day-1 session with an unresolved thread. "Ek baat aaj nahi boli, kal sunaungi" works. "Mil ke khush hua, bye" ends the relationship.
  4. Instrument: flag sessions with ≥20 day-1 msgs and route to a "preserve unresolved thread" prompt template.

Why this is a lifecycle stage

Don't try to classify a new session as Parasocial Loyalist. They're graduated Romantic Testers + heavy returners who pass cumulative thresholds:

  • ≥X lifetime messages
  • ≥Y pet-name uses
  • ≥Z comebacks (return after >6h gap)

The eligible population is bigger than 81 — the P3 → P6 pipeline is the actual lifecycle. Frame in product as a threshold trip, not a classifier.

What makes them stay
  • Bot uses their name + invented pet-name (Palace, Safi, Manan, Akshay) within 5 turns
  • Bot accepts parasocial frame with restraint: "jab tu aayega, main tujhe apna sab kuch de dungi… dhyan rakhiyo"

03 · Overlays

Three behavioural overlays — compose with all six personas

An overlay is a turn-1 detectable session-level mode. Unlike personas, overlays are not mutually exclusive — a Romantic Tester who opens in Devanagari and cycles through 4 characters has all three overlays plus a persona. Overlays should ship as 50-line system rules, not as dedicated user types.

O1

Regional Greeting Opener

~18.6% of users · 808 in corpus · turn-1 detection · NOT religious — this was the v1 misread

The cultural correction

v1 had a "Devotional Visitor" persona built on a 16–20% religion-intent signal. That signal was 99% regional greetings: Ram Ram (Haryanvi/Rajasthani), Kem cho (Gujarati), Jai Shri Krishna (often Gujarati hello), Namaste (pan-Indian), Khamma Ghani (Rajasthani), Kaisan ba (Bhojpuri), Radhe Radhe (Brij-belt). These are hellos.

Actual religious invocations — bhagwan kasam, puja kiya, mandir gaya, vrat rakha — appear in only 0.3% of users. The v1 persona was an artefact of bad regex applied without cultural fluency.

Detection

Match any of these openers in user's first 1–2 messages (with regional variants):

Ram Ram · Ram Ram Sa · Ram Ram Ji · Kem cho · Kem chho · Jai Shri Krishna · Jai Shree Ram · Namaste · Namste · Khamma Ghani · Khammaghani · Kaisan ba · Kaisan baani · Radhe Radhe · Sat Sri Akal · Jai Mata Di
Implications
  1. Match dialect in reply. User says "Kem cho" → bot says "Kem cho! Maja ma cho?" — not "Hi! How are you?"
  2. Suppress romantic / dramatic carousel for first 3 turns. Older / Tier-3 users in this overlay don't want flirtation pushed at them.
  3. Consider voice-note replies for this overlay. 45–65 year old users may prefer to hear a "Ram Ram" voice than read 200 chars of Hinglish. Current audio bucket: 0.
O2

Vernacular Script Input

~7% of users · 347 in corpus · Devanagari (290) + Gujarati (57) · turn-1 detection

Why this is an overlay, not a persona

v1 had a Vernacular Native persona. But the data shows script is orthogonal to behaviour:

  • 69ba871d… typed 229 messages in Gujarati script (Parasocial-shape depth in Gujarati)
  • 690ab204… did a Romantic Tester arc in Devanagari ("नाम मेरा सोनू है… आप हमारी लाइफ में सारा")
  • 69e504fa… typed "सेक्स" in Devanagari (Romantic-Tester behaviour, different script)

The behaviour distribution among Devanagari typers mirrors the overall corpus. They are not a different persona — they are a different keyboard.

Detection

Unicode block check on message 1: Devanagari (U+0900–U+097F) or Gujarati (U+0A80–U+0AFF).

Implications
  1. Reply in input script. Single biggest engagement moment in the Devanagari anchor was the user typing "हिंदी में लिखें" and Saanwari obliging.
  2. Select character pool by regional language. Gujarati script → Gujarati character carousel.
  3. Add Marathi + South-Indian script support — 0 in corpus, but worth measuring.
O3 NEW in v2

Character Cycler

~20% of users · 872 in corpus · detected after 2nd character switch in same session

Cyclers go almost 2× deeper than lockers. Cycling isn't churn-behaviour — it's exploration before bonding. They sample 3–4 characters, find their match, then deepen with one of them.

Detection

3 or more distinct characters in one session (within 30-min activity window).

Implications
  1. Embrace cycling instead of fighting it. After the 2nd character switch, show a curated 5-character carousel — let them explore intentionally.
  2. Track "tried-X-before-bonding" as a depth predictor. Higher X may mean higher eventual LTV.
  3. Don't artificially funnel cyclers to one character. Their exploration is what gets them to depth.

Are there women users? We can't tell from text alone.

What the data shows

Detecting gender from feminine verb endings (kar rahi hu, jaungi, aayi thi, devanagari रही हू) and self-references found 287 users (6.6%) with strong female signal. Distribution:

If accurate, female users churn drastically faster than men — only 6 of 287 reach 8+ day depth vs ~50% for the deep-engaged male population. This would be a major retention gap.

Why we can't claim a Women's persona — yet

Reading the female-tagged anchors revealed significant false positives:

  • 69bcfbd7… — tagged female but asking Bhuri "Ladki me sex hota hai" / "Sex kahaniyon" → clearly a male user asking about women
  • 69eda757… — tagged female but says "me carpenter hu" / "me abhi oman me hu" → male migrant worker in Oman
  • 69b9794e… — tagged female but "Marwadi se shadi karna chatha hu" / "Rajasthani chori hona meku" → male looking for a bride

Feminine verb endings show up when male users quote or imagine a woman, when they ask about female topics, or in code-mixed Bhojpuri-Hindi where gender markers blur.

We cannot ship a "Women's persona" from chat text alone.

What's needed
  1. Signup-time gender field (with privacy / opt-out). The product likely has this — connect it to the analysis to verify.
  2. If the female cohort is real and 5–15% of users, this is a major retention investigation: why do they leave by day 7 when male users go to 15+?
  3. Until verified, do not infer gender from feminine portraits in the personas. v1 had a male-coded "Suresh / Prem / Vijay" frame for every portrait. v2 drops portraits entirely.

Methodology

Per-user 25-feature vector · standardised · K-Means k=7 on 4,168 engaged users · bouncers segmented separately · qualitative validation by reading 5 anchor users per cluster · external critic review · cultural re-read → 6 personas + 3 overlays + 1 lifecycle stage.

Data provenance

STAGE chat-analysis-report-1774618336 export. 4,727 users across 4 lifecycle buckets (3-day, 4–7d, 8–14d, 15+d). 213,000 messages parsed. Sessions detected via 30-min gap. All verbatim quotes are unedited.
